I am the designer at Xesto, a University of Toronto accelerated computer vision startup. I designed the Xesto app and brand identity for the company, both of which launched in November 2020. The app harnesses the power of the FaceID camera in iPhones X and above to help users scan and size their feet for online shoe shopping.

Experience design:
Instructions for Xesto scanning process.

Constraint:
Use of front facing camera makes scanning process disorienting for users.

The Xesto app relies on the FaceID camera in iPhones X and up for TrueDepth scanning. Therefore, a constraint baked into the app is that the scanning process takes place with the phone screen facing away from the user.

The first step in addressing this pain point was a redesign of the instructions presented to the user. The instructions helped myself and the engineers map out the experience journey for users, examining each step in the process that would need explaining.

User testing:
Testing scanning interface with users, implementing feedback.

Feedback:
Viewfinding is harder than we thought it would be, the learning curve is high.

We then tested 3 different scanning user interfaces with users who had never been exposed to the products before. We got strong feedback that the "target" method of lining the camera up with an image of a foot was far too difficult, though multiple users had suggested it at an earlier stage.

We realized that while our first UI was too open ended, the target method was too restrictive. We asked "How can the app hold the user's hand throughout the scanning process?" and paid attention to how we would explain the scanning process when we intervened in unsuccessful user testing. This led us to the idea of a guided UI wherein Xesto checks in with the user throughout the scanning process.

User interface design:
Xesto scanning interface design.

Success:
A guided approach with multiple check-ins proved to be successful!

With the guided UI, approximately 80% of users took successful scans for the first time during our last round of testing. The key to this UI was pauses between each photograph that require the user's manual approval. "Does your photo look like this?" and a visual cue reduced error by a large margin.

I then went about designing the interface to display and interact with the scans and resulting sizing information.

Iteration:
Xesto app design iterations.

Goal:
A clean, playful interface for appropriately organizing and displaying sizing information.

We wanted the page displaying scans to be simple and playful. We experimented with different layouts but landed on one with buttons for each funtion that the user can toggle in and out of, leaving the majority of the screen free to display the foot scans.

User interface design:
Xesto scanning interface design.

Final UI:
A playful, informative UI.

The buttons toggle the left and right foot scans, in and out of photogaphic mode and into a technical sizing information screen. This method allows for a large amount of information to be hidden within a simple interface while proving fun to click in and out of.

Reflection:
Thoughts on my work with Xesto.

My time at Xesto has taught me a great deal about user-centered design and designing for successful outcomes. The constraints of the UX were clear due to the engineering team requiring use of the front facing camera. Only once we had tested, iterated and tested again did we achieve results that were within our target success rate. I learned about asking the right questions and observing users at work. Only when we framed the question as "How can the app hold the user's hand throughout the scanning process?" did we achieve the desired results.
